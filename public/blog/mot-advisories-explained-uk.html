MOT Advisories Explained – What They Mean & When to Worry (UK)

MOT advisories are notes added during an MOT test to highlight parts of a vehicle that are worn, close to limits, or likely to need attention soon. They are not MOT failures, but ignoring them often leads to future failures.

This guide explains MOT advisories in plain English — how serious they really are, when you should worry, and how to use them when buying or maintaining a car.

What is an MOT advisory?

An MOT advisory is recorded when a component is:

  • Worn but still legal
  • Deteriorating and likely to fail in the future
  • Something the tester believes should be monitored

The car passes the MOT, but the advisory acts as an early warning. It does not mean the car is unsafe today — only that attention may soon be needed.

Common MOT advisories you shouldn’t ignore

Some advisories are minor, others are early signs of bigger problems. The following are the most common MOT advisories in the UK that often turn into failures:

  • Brake pads or discs wearing thin
  • Suspension components showing wear
  • Shock absorbers misting or leaking
  • Tyres close to the legal tread limit
  • Minor oil leaks
  • Corrosion starting on structural parts

If the same advisory appears year after year, it usually means the issue has been ignored rather than repaired.

MOT advisory vs MOT failure

MOT advisory: The vehicle passes and is legal to drive, but attention is recommended.
MOT failure: The vehicle is not road legal until repaired and retested.

Many MOT failures begin life as advisories one or two years earlier. That’s why advisories matter more than people think.

Can you drive with MOT advisories?

Yes — you can legally drive a car with MOT advisories.

However, if an advisory worsens and becomes dangerous, you could still be stopped by police or face insurance issues after an accident.

An MOT advisory is not a “free pass” — it’s a warning.

Should you buy a car with MOT advisories?

Buying a car with advisories is common and often perfectly fine, if:

  • The advisories are minor wear items
  • They are not repeated every year
  • The price reflects future repair costs

A long history of repeated advisories for the same components usually points to poor maintenance.

How MOT advisories affect future MOTs

Advisories are one of the best predictors of what will fail next year’s MOT. Common progressions include:

  • Brake wear advisory → brake failure
  • Suspension wear advisory → suspension failure
  • Tyre advisory → illegal tread depth

This is why patterns across multiple MOTs matter more than a single test result.

Frequently asked questions about MOT advisories

Are MOT advisories serious?

Some are minor, others are early warnings. Repeated advisories should always be taken seriously.

Do MOT advisories affect insurance?

Not directly. However, ignoring serious defects could invalidate a claim if the fault contributed to an accident.

Do MOT advisories expire?

No. Advisories remain part of the vehicle’s MOT history permanently.

Can I clear an MOT advisory?

There is no official way to “remove” an advisory. It disappears only if the issue is repaired and does not reappear in future MOT tests.

Is it bad if a car has lots of MOT advisories?

Not always. Many older cars collect minor advisories. What matters is whether the same issues repeat every year.

Should I fix advisories before selling a car?

Fixing obvious advisories can improve buyer confidence and sale value, especially for brakes, tyres, and suspension.

Final advice

MOT advisories are not something to fear — but they are not something to ignore either. Used correctly, they help predict maintenance costs and avoid surprise MOT failures.

For the full picture, always check the complete MOT history, not just the latest result.
