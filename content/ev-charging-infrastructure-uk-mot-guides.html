What the IET Guide Actually Says — and Why Local Authorities Should Read It

The Institution of Engineering and Technology (IET) published its Guide to Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure for Local Authorities specifically to help councils plan, procure, and manage public charge point networks. The document is available to download directly from the IET's electrical guidance portal at electrical.theiet.org.

Here's the thing — many councils approach charging infrastructure reactively, installing a handful of kerb-side units and assuming the job is done. The IET guide challenges that. It walks through site assessment, grid connection capacity, tariff structures, accessibility requirements under the Equality Act 2010, and long-term maintenance obligations. These aren't optional considerations; they determine whether a charge point actually serves the community or sits idle and broken.

The IET — formally the Institution of Engineering and Technology — is a professional body with global membership and registration programmes. Its intelligence and research output, available via its Media Hub, career and learning resources, and events programme, makes it one of the most authoritative non-governmental voices on UK electrical infrastructure. If your authority hasn't downloaded a copy of the guide yet, that's the single most practical step you can take today.

How MOT Rules Apply to Electric Vehicles in the UK

Electric vehicles registered in the UK are subject to the same MOT testing timeline as petrol and diesel cars: a first test is required three years after first registration, then annually thereafter. The DVSA administers the MOT scheme, and the official MOT testing guide — published at gov.uk/guidance/mot-testing-guide — sets out the specific inspection items that apply to EVs.

In practice, EV MOT tests share most checks with conventional vehicles: lights, brakes, steering, tyres, bodywork. What differs is under the bonnet. Testers assess high-voltage warning labels, the integrity of orange high-voltage cables, and the condition of the 12V auxiliary battery. The main traction battery's health is not currently a mandatory MOT assessment item — though that may change as the fleet ages.

What often gets overlooked is how charging equipment interacts with vehicle roadworthiness. A damaged charge port or compromised onboard charger won't cause an MOT failure by itself, but associated electrical faults absolutely can. If you're unsure about a vehicle's history before purchasing, you can check your MOT history instantly using official DVSA records — it takes seconds and surfaces any past advisories related to electrical systems.

Regional Charging Gaps — the Data Picture in 2026

The honest answer is that the UK's public charging network is growing rapidly but unevenly. According to data published by Zap-Map and referenced in GOV.UK's EV infrastructure statistics, charge point density is significantly higher in London, the South East, and major urban corridors than in rural Wales, Northern Ireland, and large parts of northern England and Scotland. This isn't just inconvenient — it actively deters EV adoption in areas where domestic charging isn't feasible.

Local authorities using the IET guide can cross-reference their regional coverage against the national charge point registry, maintained by the Office for Zero Emission Vehicles (OZEV) and accessible via gov.uk. The registry shows not just quantity but connector type, charging speed (slow, fast, rapid, ultra-rapid), and operational status.

For private drivers trying to find the right electric car for their lifestyle, regional charging coverage should factor into the decision — not just the vehicle's stated range. A 300-mile range is irrelevant if your nearest rapid charger is 40 miles away and frequently out of service.

The Gap Competitors Miss: MOT Advisories as an EV Infrastructure Signal

Here's an insight you won't find in the IET guide, the HAUC-UK documentation, or most council planning frameworks: aggregated MOT advisory data is one of the most underused signals for predicting where EV charging demand will cluster in the near future.

Autodun's analysis of DVSA MOT records shows that advisory rates for tyre wear, brake degradation, and suspension issues cluster geographically in ways that correlate closely with high-mileage commuter routes. Those same routes — where drivers are racking up miles daily — are exactly where rapid charging demand will be highest as EV penetration increases.

Practically speaking, a local authority that maps MOT advisory frequency against its current charge point locations will often find misalignment: charging is concentrated in town centres and supermarket car parks, while high-mileage drivers on suburban commuter routes have almost no provision. The IET guide covers demand modelling, but it doesn't have access to real-world vehicle usage data at this level of granularity. That's a gap worth bridging.

Common Mistakes in EV Infrastructure Planning — and How to Avoid Them

Planning teams consistently make the same errors. The first is treating charge point installation as a one-off capital project rather than an ongoing service. Charge points require maintenance contracts, software updates, and payment system management. Councils that procure hardware without securing long-term service agreements often find units offline within 18 months.

The second mistake is ignoring dwell time. Rapid chargers belong at motorway services and retail parks where drivers stop for 20–45 minutes. Slow 7kW chargers belong at residential streets and workplace car parks where dwell time is several hours. Installing rapid chargers in the wrong locations wastes capital and frustrates drivers.

Third — and this catches many authorities — is grid capacity. A single 150kW rapid charger draws as much power as a small street of houses. The IET guide's section on grid connection is not light reading, but skipping it is how projects stall at planning stage. OZEV's guidance, available at gov.uk/government/organisations/office-for-zero-emission-vehicles, sets out the grant landscape for councils looking to offset connection costs.

What the Official Guidance Says

Two sources are essential reading for anyone working on EV charging infrastructure in the UK. First, the IET's Guide to Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure for Local Authorities, available at electrical.theiet.org — this is the industry standard for council-level planning. Second, DVSA's official MOT testing guide at gov.uk/guidance/mot-testing-guide, which covers EV-specific inspection items. For grant funding and national strategy, OZEV's pages at gov.uk remain the authoritative source. Always verify current requirements directly with these bodies before making procurement or compliance decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do electric vehicles need an MOT in the UK?

Yes. Electric vehicles registered in the UK follow the same MOT schedule as any other car — the first test falls three years after first registration, then annually. The DVSA sets out EV-specific inspection items in its official MOT testing guide at gov.uk, including checks on high-voltage cable integrity and warning labels. The main traction battery is not currently a mandatory test item, but associated electrical systems are. If you're unsure when your EV's MOT is due, you can check your MOT history free using official DVSA data.

What is the IET Guide to Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure for Local Authorities?

The IET — Institution of Engineering and Technology — published this guide to help UK local authorities plan, install, and manage public EV charging networks. It covers site selection, grid connection, accessibility, tariff structures, and maintenance obligations. Councils can download a copy of the Guide to Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure for Local Authorities directly from the IET's electrical guidance portal. The IET supports this work through its membership and registration framework, career and learning resources, intelligence and research output, and events programme. It's the definitive technical reference for council procurement teams.

Where are the biggest EV charging gaps in the UK?

Rural areas — particularly in Wales, Northern Ireland, and parts of northern England and Scotland — have significantly fewer charge points per driver than London and the South East. The national charge point registry, maintained by OZEV and accessible via gov.uk, maps current provision by connector type and speed. The honest reality is that ultra-rapid charging remains concentrated on motorway corridors, leaving suburban commuter routes and market towns underserved. Local authorities can use the IET guide's demand modelling methodology alongside the national registry to identify specific gaps in their area.

Can a faulty charge port cause an MOT failure?

Not directly — a damaged charge port alone isn't an MOT failure item. That said, electrical faults associated with the charging system can cause failures if they affect lighting, warning systems, or create visible damage to high-voltage components. Testers check that all high-voltage cables are correctly routed, labelled, and undamaged. If you have concerns about your EV's electrical health before an MOT, it's worth having it assessed by a qualified EV technician. For historical advisory data on any vehicle, check your MOT history to see what testers have flagged in previous years.
